Why "Weekend" Is Actually Possible

Most SaaS MVPs fail before they launch — not because the idea is bad, but because the founder runs out of steam wiring up auth and payments before writing a single line of product code. The technical core of a SaaS is 90% the same as every other SaaS:

  • Sign up / log in (email + OAuth)
  • Protect routes behind auth
  • Collect payment via Stripe Checkout
  • Let users manage their subscription in a billing portal
  • Send transactional emails (welcome, receipts, password reset)
  • Handle webhook events from Stripe to update DB

These six things take 2–3 weeks to build from scratch with tests, error handling, and edge cases. A good boilerplate has all of it pre-built and pre-tested. Your job becomes customizing the landing page, wiring up your specific product features, and deploying.

Here's how to allocate your weekend for maximum shipped-ness:

Hour 0–1: Pick your stack and clone

Hour 12–48: Distribution
Post on Twitter/X with a demo. Submit to directories (Launching Next, SaaSHub). Post on LinkedIn if your audience is B2B. Reply to every comment. Drive waitlist signups and first paying customers.

Where Most Tutorials Lose You

Most "build a SaaS in a weekend" tutorials stop at the landing page. They hand you a Stripe test mode and call it done. What they skip:

  • Webhook security — verifying Stripe signatures in production, retry logic for failed webhooks, idempotency keys
  • Customer portal — letting users upgrade, downgrade, or cancel without emailing you
  • OAuth session management — what happens when a Google OAuth token expires mid-session
  • Row-Level Security — making sure User A cannot read User B's data in Supabase

Distribution on Day One

You've deployed. Now what? A deployed SaaS with no users is just a hobby project. Here's the day-one distribution checklist:

  1. Post on X/Twitter — Short demo video, one-liner value prop, link to live product. Screenshare your Stripe dashboard at €1 MRR. People love watching new things get their first customers.
  2. Submit to directories — Launching Next, SaaSHub, AlternativeTo. These drive durable SEO traffic, not just one-time spikes.
  3. Post on LinkedIn — If your SaaS solves a B2B problem, LinkedIn posts with a demo perform well. Frame it as "I built X in a weekend" — people engage with builder stories.
  4. Find your 10 early users manually — DM people on Twitter who have the problem your SaaS solves. Don't sell. Ask questions. Get feedback. Convert the ones who say "I need this" into paying customers.

What to Build After Your First €100 MRR

Once you have paying users, the roadmap becomes clear — they'll tell you what to build next. Before that point, resist the urge to add features. The MVP is done when:

  • One person can sign up, pay, and get value from the core feature
  • The billing works reliably (webhooks fire, DB updates, receipts arrive)
  • The onboarding flow takes a new user from zero to "this works" in under 5 minutes

Everything else is a distraction until you hit €100 MRR. Then you have real data to decide what to build next.
